Introduction

Background

Sleep is a widely conserved feature across animal species, imperative for life. Many studies have highlighted the importance of sleep, for example by investigating the effect of sleep amount on survival [1][2], but the regulation of sleep is poorly understood. Equally, the function of sleep is not explicitly defined, although it is intuitively established to have a restorative effect.

In spite of being poorly understood phenomenon, sleep timing is believed to be tightly regulated by two primary interacting processes: circadian rhythm and homeostatic process [3] . Although extensive research into circadian rhythms has been conducted, relatively little is known about the homeostatic part of sleep regulation. Moreover, sleep-wake cycles are also influenced by hundreds of environmental and genetic interactions [4]. To reduce some of this complexity, Drosophila melanogaster flie used as a model organism to understand mechanisms that underlie sleep and translate these findings to human sleep.

Drosophila flies have been used extensively in sleep research; both to further understanding of sleep and to act as a model of human sleep [5]. They share homologous sleep features with humans, such as a stable circadian sleep rhythm (see Fig.1) and experiencing longer episodes of rebound after sleep deprivation [6]. Moreover, flies have similar responses to stimulant substances that would typically affect human sleep, such as caffeine and cocaine [7][8]. Using Drosophila enables monitoring over a longer period (>4 days) than in mammalian sleep duration studies (typically 1 day) [9] and allows for a greater number of transitions available for statistical analysis than mammalian sleep studies can offer, making Drosophila flies an advantageous model organism for human sleep.
